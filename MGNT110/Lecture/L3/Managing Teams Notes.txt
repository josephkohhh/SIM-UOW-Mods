Managing Teams & Groups

Team vs Group
- A team is 2 more ppl psychologically contracted tgt to 
  achieve shared goals, and share responsibility and accountability for 
  outcomes 
  - a psychological contract = informal obligation between 1 person
    and another or within a group 

- A group has a shared goal but 
  - no psychologically contract is involved
  - no interdependency is assumed 
  

In summary:
Team 
- shared goal
- psychological contract
- interpendence
- shared accountability

Ex. students working on an assignment (depends on each other)

Group
- shared goal
- no psychological contract
- no interpendence (dont rely on each other)

Ex. students sitting tgt but doing own work 


Why Group? 
- Ppl join group to have sense of safety/belonging

1. Group Types
   - formal (work teams)
   - informal (friends)
   - open vs closed (exclusive)
     - open = flexible, easy entry/exit | closed = selective, limited entry/exit

2. Group Properties
   - size (small vs large)
   - closeness (friends vs strangers)
   - strength of relationship 

3. Impact on Performance
   - social facilitation
     - "ppl perform better when others are watching"

4. Conformance & obedience 
   - ppl follow group even if wrong
     - everyone agrees in meeting even if they disagree



Challenges in Groups

1. Groupthink (Janis 1972)
- Everyone agrees too quickly and no one challenge ideas
  - desire for consensus within a group overrides individuals'
    motivation to critically evaluate alternative ideas/viewpoints 

Groupthink leads to 
• Poor decisions
• No critical thinking
• No innovation

To fix it
• Encourage opinions
• Allow disagreement
• Promote critique
• Reflect on decisions


2. Social loafing (Latané, Williams and Harkins 1979)
- Ppl put in less effort in a group compared to working alone 
  - due to reduced accountability and diffusion of responsibility 
    ("someone else will do it mindset")

Social loafing leads to 
• Unfair workload
• Low performance
• Team conflict

How to fix it:
• Clear roles
• Clear goals
• Accountability


Not all teams are the same. 3 key dimensions to classify them
1. Skill Differentiation
  - High Skill differentiation (each member has unique expertise, hard to replace)
  - Low Skill differentiation (members have similar skills, easily replaceable)

2. Authority Differentiation
   - Traditional company → manager decides
   - Startup team → everyone contributes

3. Temporal Stability
   - High Stability
     - long-term team
     - strong relationships
     - high trust
   - Low Stability
     - temporary team
     - project-based
     - less bonding 


Stages of group development (not all groups go through same stage)
1. Forming (Orientation phase)
   - Team members first come tgt
   - Ppl are polite, cautious, and unsure of their roles
   - Focus is on understanding goals and getting oriented
   👉 Think: “We’re just getting to know each other.”

2. Storming (onflict phase)
   - Conflicts and disagreements start to appear
   - Members challenge ideas, roles, or leadership
   - This stage can feel uncomfortable but is important for growth
   👉 Think: “We don’t all agree yet.”

3. Norming (Emergence phase)
   - The team begins to resolve conflicts
   - Roles and rules become clearer
   - Trust and cooperation increase
   👉 Think: “We’re starting to work well together.”

4. Performing (Reinforcement phase)
   - The team functions effectively and efficiently
   - Members collaborate smoothly toward shared goals
   - High productivity and strong relationships
   👉 Think: “We’re working as a solid team.”

5. Adjourning (Disbanding phase)
   - The team disbands after completing its task
   - Members reflect on achievements and may feel a sense of closure or loss
   - Destructive role-playing may become more prevalent 
   👉 Think: “We’ve finished—time to wrap up.”


* Not all groups go through the 5 stages due to
 - Be ‘immortal’
 - Move between different stages
 - Self-destruct before reaching stages 3–5
 - Have no storming phase at all


Managing Team Conflict
7 deadly INs
- Intention (When someone deliberately tries to hurt others)
- Incompetence (Lack of skills or ability)
- Infidelity (Betrayal of trust)
- Insensitivity (Lack of awareness of others’ feelings)
- Intrusion (Invading personal space or boundaries)
- Institutional forces (Conflict caused by system or org)
- Inevitability (Conflict is unavoidable)

How managers should respond?
Instead of: 
 – blaming individuals

They should:
– clarify roles
– improve communication
– manage expectations
– support team members
