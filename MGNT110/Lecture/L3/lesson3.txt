Managing teams and groups 

Team:
- 2 or more people psychologically contracted
together to achieve common goals, share
responsibility and accountability for outcomes
– A psychological contract refers to the informal
obligations between one person and another or
within a group.

Group:
A group differs from a team
– Shared goal but no psychological contract is involved
– No interdependency is assumed


Group
Why group? - safety/belonging
Ex. new employee joins group to feel included

Group types
- formal (work teams)
- informal (friends)
- open vs closed (exclusive)

Group properties 
- Size (small vs large)
- Closeness (friends vs strangers)
- Strength of relationship 

Impact on performance
- social facilitation
  - psychologist say people perform better when being watched

Conformance & Obedience
- People follow group even if wrong
  - Everyone agree in meeting even if they disagree

Challenges in groups
Groupthink (Janis 1972)
- Everyone agrees too quickly
-  No one challenges ideas

Social loafing (Latané, Williams and Harkins 1979)
- People put in less effort in a group
“Someone else will do it” mindset

Groupthink leads to 
• Poor decisions
• No critical thinking
• No innovation

To fix it
• Encourage opinions
• Allow disagreement
• Promote critique
• Reflect on decisions


Social loafing leads to 
• Unfair workload
• Low performance
• Team conflict

How to fix it:
• Clear roles
• Clear goals
• Accountability


Not all teams are same
3 Key dimensions:
- Skill differentiation
  - High Skill differentiation (each member has unique expertise, hard to replace)
  - Low Skill differentiation (members have similar skills, easily replaceable)
- Authority Differentiation
  - Who has decision making power
    - Traditional company → manager decides
    - Startup team → everyone contributes
- Temporal Stability
  how long team member work together 
  - High Stability
    - long term team / strong relationships / high trust
  - Low Stability 
    - temporary team / project-based / less bonding 


The Tuckman Model (Tuckman & Jensen, 1977) 
It explains how teams typically develop and function over time. 
It suggests that groups go through 5 predictable stages as they work together:
1. Forming (Orientation phase)
   - Team members first come together
   - People are polite, cautious, and unsure of their roles
   - Focus is on understanding goals and getting oriented
   👉 Think: “We’re just getting to know each other.”
2. Storming (Conflict phase)
   - Conflicts and disagreements start to appear
   - Members challenge ideas, roles, or leadership
   - This stage can feel uncomfortable but is important for growth
   👉 Think: “We don’t all agree yet.”
3. Norming (Emergence phase)
   - The team begins to resolve conflicts
   - Roles and rules become clearer
   - Trust and cooperation increase
   👉 Think: “We’re starting to work well together.”
4. Performing (Reinforcement phase)
   - The team functions effectively and efficiently
   - Members collaborate smoothly toward shared goals
   - High productivity and strong relationships
   👉 Think: “We’re working as a solid team.”
5. Adjourning (Disbanding phase)
   - The team disbands after completing its task
   - Members reflect on achievements and may feel a sense of closure or loss
   👉 Think: “We’ve finished—time to wrap up.”


Managing team conflict
The Seven Deadly INs
- Intention (When someone deliberately tries to hurt others)
- Incompetence (Lack of skills or ability)
- Infidelity (Betrayal of trust)
- Insensitivity (Lack of awareness of others’ feelings)
- Intrusion (Invading personal space or boundaries)
- Institutional forces (Conflict caused by system or org)
- Inevitability (Conflict is unavoidable)

How managers should respond?
Instead of: 
 – blaming individuals

They should:
– clarify roles
– improve communication
– manage expectations
– support team members



Summary 
• Teams are an important form
  – stimulate human relations that are productive, cohesive and aligned to organizational outcomes.
• Group dynamics affect performance
  – influenced by dynamics such as group size, cohesion and social facilitation, conformance and obedience.
• Teams develop through different stages
  – depending on what organizational tasks they are formed to work on.
• Managing teams is not just about structure; it’s about managing behaviour, relationships, and conflict.
